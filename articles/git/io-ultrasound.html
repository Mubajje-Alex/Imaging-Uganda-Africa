Ultrasound of Bowel Obstruction

Introduction

Bowel obstruction is a common surgical emergency where ultrasound serves as an excellent first-line imaging modality due to its real-time assessment capability, lack of radiation, and ability to evaluate peristalsis. This article covers the sonographic features of different types of bowel obstruction with emphasis on distinguishing mechanical obstruction from paralytic ileus and identifying closed loop obstruction.

Key Advantages of Ultrasound in Bowel Obstruction:

  • Real-time evaluation of peristalsis (critical for differentiating mechanical vs paralytic obstruction)
  • No ionizing radiation (important for repeated examinations in pediatric and pregnant patients)
  • Bedside availability (useful in unstable patients)
  • Ability to detect extraluminal complications (free fluid, abscesses)
  • Dynamic assessment with compression (evaluates bowel loop distensibility)

1. Mechanical Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)

Results from physical blockage of the intestinal lumen, most commonly due to adhesions (60%), hernias (15%), or neoplasms (15%).

Pathophysiology

Obstruction → proximal bowel distension → fluid/air accumulation → increased intraluminal pressure → compromised venous return → ischemia → perforation.

Ultrasound Findings

  • Dilated small bowel loops (>2.5cm diameter)
  • Increased peristalsis early (decreased/absent in late stages)
  • Fluid-filled loops with "to-and-fro" movement of contents
  • Wall thickening (>3mm) in ischemic cases
  • Transition point (abrupt caliber change) may be visible
  • Free fluid between loops (suggests strangulation)

Small Bowel Obstruction

  • Multiple dilated fluid-filled loops
  • Wall-to-wall apposition (arrows)
  • Increased peristalsis

Transition Point

  • Abrupt caliber change (arrow)
  • Proximal dilation
  • Collapsed distal bowel

2. Mechanical Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)

Typically caused by carcinoma (60%), volvulus (15%), or diverticular disease (10%). Ultrasound has lower sensitivity than for SBO due to frequent gas distension.

Ultrasound Findings

  • Colonic dilation (>6cm cecum, >8cm elsewhere)
  • Haustral markings visible in distended colon
  • Lack of peristalsis (colon normally has minimal peristalsis)
  • Mass lesion may be visible at obstruction site
  • Pseudo-kidney sign in annular lesions
  • Closed loop configuration in volvulus

Large Bowel Obstruction

  • Markedly dilated colon
  • Visible haustra
  • Minimal peristalsis

Obstructing Mass

  • Hypoechoic mass
  • Proximal dilation
  • "Pseudo-kidney" appearance

3. Paralytic Ileus

Functional obstruction due to impaired peristalsis without mechanical blockage, commonly postoperative or due to electrolyte imbalance, peritonitis, or medications.

Ultrasound Findings

  • Diffuse bowel dilation (both small and large bowel)
  • Absent/minimal peristalsis (key differentiating feature)
  • Fluid-filled loops with static contents
  • No transition point
  • Normal bowel wall thickness (unless secondary ischemia develops)
  • Possible free fluid depending on underlying cause

Paralytic Ileus

  • Diffuse bowel dilation
  • Static fluid-filled loops
  • Absent peristalsis

Peristalsis Assessment

  • M-mode shows flat line
  • No peristaltic waves
  • Static bowel contents

4. Closed Loop Obstruction

A surgical emergency where a bowel segment is obstructed at two points along its course, often leading to volvulus and rapid progression to ischemia.

Ultrasound Findings

  • U-shaped or C-shaped dilated loop with converging ends
  • Whirl sign (twisted mesentery with swirling vessels)
  • Wall thickening (>4mm) with stratification loss
  • Decreased/absent peristalsis in affected segment
  • Mesenteric edema (hyperechoic fat)
  • Free fluid (often complex with debris)
  • Absent color Doppler signal in ischemic segments

Closed Loop Configuration

  • C-shaped dilated loop (L)
  • Converging ends (arrows)
  • Mesenteric edema

Whirl Sign

  • Swirling mesenteric vessels
  • Twisted mesentery
  • Pathognomonic for volvulus

5. Comparative Ultrasound Features

Feature Mechanical SBO Mechanical LBO Paralytic Ileus Closed Loop
Bowel Diameter >2.5cm (small bowel) >6-8cm (colon) Diffuse dilation Localized U/C-shaped
Peristalsis Hyperperistalsis early Minimal (normal colon) Absent Absent in loop
Transition Point Present Present Absent Two points
Wall Thickness Normal unless ischemic Normal unless tumor Normal Thickened (>4mm)
Pathognomonic Sign To-and-fro movement Pseudo-kidney sign Static fluid levels Whirl sign
Diagnostic Tip: The combination of bowel loop configuration, peristalsis assessment, and presence/absence of transition point allows reliable differentiation between obstruction types. Always evaluate for signs of ischemia (wall thickening, absent flow, free fluid) which mandate urgent surgical intervention.
