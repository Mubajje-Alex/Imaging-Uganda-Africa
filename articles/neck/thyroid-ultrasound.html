Neck & Thyroid Ultrasound

Introduction

Neck ultrasound is the primary imaging modality for evaluating thyroid and parathyroid pathology, as well as cervical lymph nodes and other neck structures. High-resolution ultrasound provides detailed assessment of thyroid nodules, inflammatory conditions, and malignancies. Doppler evaluation adds functional information about vascular patterns that helps differentiate benign from malignant lesions. In Africa, where thyroid disorders are prevalent due to both iodine deficiency and autoimmune conditions, ultrasound plays a crucial role in diagnosis and management.

Key advantages of thyroid ultrasound include:

  • No ionizing radiation exposure
  • Real-time imaging with high spatial resolution
  • Ability to guide fine needle aspiration biopsies
  • Assessment of vascular patterns with Doppler
  • Evaluation of adjacent structures (lymph nodes, parathyroids)

Neck Anatomy

Normal Thyroid:

  • Homogeneous medium-level echogenicity (slightly hyperechoic to strap muscles)
  • Butterfly shape with isthmus connecting two lobes
  • Normal measurements: Lobe length 4-6cm, AP 1.5-2cm, width 2-2.5cm
  • Isthmus thickness: ≤5mm
  • Normal vascularity on Doppler (mainly inferior thyroid arteries)

Adjacent Structures:

  • Trachea: Hyperechoic anterior wall with posterior shadowing
  • Common carotid artery: Lateral to thyroid lobe
  • Internal jugular vein: Lateral to carotid, compressible
  • Parathyroid glands: Normally not visible, may be seen when enlarged
  • Cervical lymph nodes: Oval shape with fatty hilum when normal

Clinical Indications

1. Common Indications

  • Evaluation of palpable neck mass
  • Assessment of thyroid nodules
  • Follow-up of known thyroid disease
  • Guidance for fine needle aspiration biopsy
  • Evaluation of suspected thyroiditis

2. Specific Clinical Scenarios

  • Thyroid nodules: Risk stratification using TI-RADS
  • Thyroid cancer: Evaluation of primary tumor and lymph nodes
  • Graves' disease: Hypervascular "thyroid inferno" pattern
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis: Heterogeneous, hypoechoic parenchyma
  • Parathyroid adenoma: Hypoechoic oval lesion posterior to thyroid


Scanning Technique

1. Patient Preparation

  • Supine position with neck extended (pillow under shoulders)
  • Remove neck jewelry and clothing
  • Use ample gel for good transducer contact
  • Explain swallowing maneuvers if needed

2. Equipment Settings

  • Transducer: High-frequency linear (7-15 MHz)
  • Depth: Adjust to include entire thyroid and adjacent structures
  • Harmonic imaging: Improves tissue contrast
  • Doppler settings: PRF 800-1500 Hz for thyroid vessels
  • Color gain: Adjust to detect parenchymal flow

3. Scanning Approach

  • Systematic survey of both lobes in transverse and longitudinal planes
  • Document isthmus thickness
  • Evaluate for nodules (location, size, characteristics)
  • Assess vascularity with color Doppler
  • Examine cervical lymph nodes (levels II-VI)
  • Measure any abnormal findings in 3 dimensions

Normal Sonographic Findings

1. Thyroid Appearance

  • Homogeneous medium-level echogenicity
  • Fine echotexture without discrete nodules
  • Thin hyperechoic capsule
  • Normal vascularity on Doppler (mild flow)

2. Variants

  • Pyramidal lobe: Extends superiorly from isthmus (15-30% of people)
  • Thyroglossal duct remnants: Midline cystic structures
  • Ectopic thyroid tissue: Lingual thyroid, etc.
  • Simple cysts: Rare in thyroid, more common in parathyroids

Pathological Findings

1. Thyroid Nodules (TI-RADS Classification)

  • Benign features: Cystic, spongiform, comet-tail artifacts
  • Suspicious features: Hypoechoic, taller-than-wide, irregular margins
  • Malignant features: Microcalcifications, extrathyroidal extension
  • Doppler patterns: Peripheral vs. chaotic internal vascularity

2. Diffuse Thyroid Disease

  • Graves' disease: Enlarged gland with hypervascular "thyroid inferno"
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis: Heterogeneous, hypoechoic with fibrous septa
  • Subacute thyroiditis: Focal hypoechoic areas with tender enlargement
  • Multinodular goiter: Multiple nodules of varying size and echogenicity

3. Thyroid Malignancies

  • Papillary carcinoma: Hypoechoic with microcalcifications (most common)
  • Medullary carcinoma: Coarse calcifications, often familial
  • Anaplastic carcinoma: Rapidly growing, invasive margins
  • Lymphoma: Markedly hypoechoic, pseudocystic appearance

4. Parathyroid Pathology

  • Adenoma: Hypoechoic oval lesion with polar vessel
  • Hyperplasia: Multiple enlarged glands
  • Carcinoma: Large irregular mass with invasive features

5. Cervical Lymphadenopathy

  • Reactive nodes: Preserved fatty hilum, oval shape
  • Metastatic nodes: Round, loss of hilum, cystic/calcified changes
  • Tuberculous nodes: Matting, necrotic centers, adjacent soft tissue changes
