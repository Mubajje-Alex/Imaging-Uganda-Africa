Pancreatic Ultrasound

Introduction

Pancreatic ultrasound serves as the initial imaging modality for pancreatic evaluation due to its:

  • Non-invasive nature and absence of ionizing radiation
  • Real-time imaging capabilities
  • Ability to assess vascular flow with Doppler
  • Cost-effectiveness compared to CT/MRI

Limitations: Often limited by overlying bowel gas, with reported visualization rates of 50-90% for the pancreatic head and 25-50% for the body/tail.

Pancreatic Anatomy

Location: Retroperitoneal organ at L1-L2 level, divided into:

  • Head: Nestled in C-loop of duodenum
  • Uncinate process: Posterior extension of head
  • Neck: Anterior to SMV/SMA
  • Body: Anterior to aorta and left renal vein
  • Tail: Extends to splenic hilum

Size: Varies with age (decreases with aging):

  • Head: 2.0-3.5 cm
  • Body: 1.5-2.5 cm
  • Tail: 1.0-2.0 cm

Ductal System: Main pancreatic duct (Wirsung) normally <3 mm in head, <2 mm in body/tail



Clinical Indications

1. Common Indications

  • Epigastric/left upper quadrant pain
  • Suspected acute/chronic pancreatitis
  • Evaluation of pancreatic masses
  • Jaundice workup
  • Follow-up of known pancreatic lesions

2. Specific Clinical Scenarios

  • Acute pancreatitis: Severe epigastric pain + elevated amylase/lipase
  • Chronic pancreatitis: Recurrent pain + steatorrhea + calcifications
  • Pancreatic cancer: Painless jaundice + weight loss
  • Cystic lesions: Incidental findings on other imaging

Scanning Technique

1. Patient Preparation

  • Fasting: 6-8 hours to reduce bowel gas
  • Positioning: Supine, then right/left oblique as needed
  • Respiration: Deep inspiration often helpful

2. Equipment Settings

  • Transducer: Curvilinear (3-5 MHz) for adults, higher frequency for pediatrics
  • Depth: Adjust to include entire pancreas and adjacent vessels
  • Harmonic imaging: Improves tissue contrast
  • Compound imaging: Reduces speckle artifact

3. Scanning Approach

  • Transverse scans: Identify SMA/SMV axis, then trace pancreas
  • Longitudinal scans: Assess head/uncinate through liver
  • Tail visualization: Often requires splenic window
  • Dynamic maneuvers: Patient rotation, upright positioning

Normal Sonographic Findings

1. Pancreatic Parenchyma

  • Homogeneous, slightly hyperechoic to liver (in adults)
  • More hypoechoic in children/young adults
  • Normal size as described in anatomy section

2. Pancreatic Duct

  • Thin echogenic walls with anechoic lumen
  • Gradually tapers from head to tail
  • No abrupt caliber changes

3. Vascular Landmarks

  • SMA/SMV: Anterior to uncinate process
  • Splenic vein: Along posterior pancreatic surface
  • Common bile duct: Posterior to pancreatic head

Pathological Findings

1. Acute Pancreatitis

  • Findings: Hypoechoic, enlarged pancreas with indistinct margins
  • Complications: Peripancreatic fluid collections, necrosis (heterogeneous areas)
  • Severity signs: Vascular thrombosis, extrapancreatic spread

2. Chronic Pancreatitis

  • Parenchymal changes: Heterogeneous, atrophic with hyperechoic foci (calcifications)
  • Ductal changes: Irregular dilatation (>3mm), intraductal calculi
  • Pseudocysts: Well-defined anechoic collections with debris

3. Pancreatic Cysts

  • Simple cysts: Anechoic, thin-walled, no septations
  • Pseudocysts: Thick-walled, debris, history of pancreatitis
  • Mucinous neoplasms: Thick septations, mural nodules
  • Serous cystadenoma: Microcystic "honeycomb" appearance

4. Pancreatic Adenocarcinoma

  • Direct signs: Hypoechoic mass with irregular margins
  • Secondary signs: Pancreatic/bile duct dilation, vascular encasement
  • Metastases: Liver lesions, lymphadenopathy

5. Neuroendocrine Tumors

  • Well-circumscribed, round, hypoechoic masses
  • Hypervascular on Doppler
  • May be multiple in MEN-1 syndrome

6. Congenital Anomalies

  • Pancreas divisum: Non-fusion of dorsal/ventral ducts
  • Annular pancreas: Pancreatic tissue encircling duodenum
  • Ectopic pancreas: Most common in stomach/duodenum
