Scrotal Ultrasound

Introduction

Diagnostic ultrasound is the most common imaging technique used to supplement the physical examination of the scrotum and is an accurate means of evaluating many scrotal processes. Technical advancements in high-resolution real-time sonography and the ability of color and power Doppler sonography to evaluate testicular perfusion have improved and expanded the clinical applications of scrotal sonography to include (among other indications) assessment of scrotal masses, evaluation of acute scrotal pain, evaluation of scrotal trauma, assessment of varicoceles in the infertility workup, assessment for tumors and metastatic disease, and evaluation of an undescended testis.

Scrotal Anatomy

Normal Structures:

  • Testis: Homogeneous medium-level echogenicity, 3-5 cm length
  • Epididymis: Head (10-12 mm), body and tail, iso/hypoechoic to testis
  • Tunica albuginea: Hyperechoic outer covering
  • Mediastinum testis: Echogenic band in sagittal plane
  • Appendix testis/epididymis: Small ovoid structures

Normal Measurements:

  • Testis volume: 15-25 mL (L×W×H×0.52)
  • Testicular length: 3-5 cm
  • Epididymal head: ≤12 mm
  • Pampiniform plexus veins: ≤2 mm diameter

Clinical Indications

1. Common Indications

  • Acute scrotal pain (torsion, epididymitis)
  • Palpable scrotal mass
  • Testicular trauma
  • Infertility evaluation
  • Undescended testis localization

2. Specific Clinical Scenarios

  • Testicular torsion: Acute pain, absent cremasteric reflex
  • Epididymo-orchitis: Pain, swelling, fever
  • Testicular cancer: Painless mass, heaviness
  • Varicocele: Scrotal swelling, infertility
  • Hydrocele: Painless scrotal enlargement


Scanning Technique

1. Patient Preparation

  • No specific preparation needed
  • Warm gel for patient comfort
  • Support scrotum with towel if needed
  • Privacy considerations important

2. Equipment Settings

  • Transducer: High-frequency linear (7-15 MHz)
  • Depth: Adjust to include entire testis and epididymis
  • Harmonic imaging: Improves tissue contrast
  • Doppler settings: Low PRF (500-800 Hz) for testicular vessels
  • Color gain: Adjust to detect slow flow

3. Scanning Approach

  • Systematic survey of both testes in two planes
  • Compare echotexture and vascularity bilaterally
  • Evaluate epididymis in longitudinal and transverse
  • Assess for hydrocele and varicocele with Valsalva
  • Document measurements of any abnormalities

Normal Sonographic Findings

1. Testis Appearance

  • Homogeneous medium-level echogenicity
  • Smooth tunica albuginea
  • Mediastinum testis as echogenic band
  • Normal vascularity on color Doppler

2. Epididymis

  • Head: 10-12 mm, slightly hyperechoic
  • Body and tail: 2-4 mm diameter
  • Less vascular than testis on Doppler

3. Variants

  • Appendix testis: Small ovoid structure superior pole
  • Testicular cysts: Small, anechoic, no vascularity
  • Epididymal cysts: Common in head
  • Testicular microlithiasis: Tiny echogenic foci

Pathological Findings

1. Testicular Torsion

  • Decreased/absent testicular flow on Doppler
  • Enlarged heterogeneous testis in later stages
  • Twisted spermatic cord ("whirlpool sign")
  • Reactive hydrocele

2. Epididymo-orchitis

  • Enlarged hyperemic epididymis (usually head first)
  • Increased testicular vascularity in orchitis
  • Reactive hydrocele
  • Possible abscess formation

3. Testicular Tumors

  • Focal hypoechoic or mixed echogenicity mass
  • Increased vascularity in most malignancies
  • Possible calcifications or cystic areas
  • Extratesticular extension in advanced cases

4. Varicocele

  • Dilated pampiniform plexus veins (>2 mm)
  • Venous flow increasing with Valsalva
  • Most common on left side
  • Grade 1-3 based on dilation extent

5. Hydrocele

  • Anechoic fluid surrounding testis
  • No internal vascularity
  • Possible septations in chronic cases
  • Testis pushed posteriorly

6. Trauma

  • Testicular fracture: Disrupted tunica albuginea
  • Hematoma: Mixed echogenicity collection
  • Hematocele: Complex hydrocele
  • Disrupted vascularity in severe cases
