Portal Hypertension

Introduction

Portal hypertension is a clinical syndrome characterized by an abnormal increase in portal venous pressure, typically defined as a hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG) greater than 5 mmHg.

Pathophysiologically, portal hypertension can be divided into presinusoidal and intrahepatic groups, depending on whether the hepatic vein wedge pressure is normal (presinusoidal) or elevated (intrahepatic).

  1. Presinusoidal portal hypertension occurs when the obstruction is located before the liver sinusoids, either in the portal vein (prehepatic) or within the portal tracts (intrahepatic).
  2. Intrahepatic portal hypertension results from increased resistance to portal blood flow within the liver parenchyma.

Portal hypertension is most commonly caused by liver cirrhosis but can also result from prehepatic (e.g., portal vein thrombosis) or posthepatic (e.g., Budd-Chiari syndrome) conditions.
Cirrhosis causes buildup of scar tissue which obstructs normal blood flow through the liver, causing a buildup of pressure in the portal veins. Difuse metastatic liver disease also produces portal hypertension by the same mechanism.

Ultrasound features

The sonographic indings of portal hypertension include the secondary signs of;

1. Splenomegaly
2. Ascites
3. Portosystemic venous collaterals.
These forms when the resistance to blood low in the portal vessels exceeds the resistance to low in the small communicating channels between the portal and systemic circulations.
4. Increase in portal vein caliber (>1.3cm)
Although the caliber of the portal vein initially may be increased in portal hypertension, with the development of portosystemic shunts, the portal vein caliber will decrease

Five major sites of portosystemic venous collaterals are visualized by ultrasound

Gastroesophageal junction.
Between the coronary and short gastric veins and the systemic esophageal veins.
Paraumbilical vein.
Runs in the falciform ligament and connects the let portal vein to the systemic epigastric veins near the umbilicus.
Splenorenal and gastrorenal.
Tortuous veins may be seen in the region of the splenic and let renal hilus, which represent collaterals between the splenic, coronary, and short gastric veins and the let adrenal or renal veins.
Intestinal.
Regions in which the gastrointestinal tract becomes retroperitoneal so that the veins of the ascending and descending colon, duodenum, pancreas, and liver may anastomose with the renal, phrenic, and lumbar veins
Hemorrhoidal.
The perianal region where the superior rectal veins, which extend from the inferior mesenteric vein, anastomose with the systemic middle and inferior rectal veins
